Chapter 1: The ST Stacking Timeline and Anatomy

To master ST Stacking, you must first master its Time and Space!

 

Ideally, ST Stacking can last forever. Given Perfect Play, there is probably no end to it. So lets see how it works!

 

This is the timeline for ST Stacking. Each ST Stack starts off with an Opener, which quickly transitions to the Midgame. ST Stacking will spend the bulk of its time in the Midgame. We will split the Midgame into 2 smaller sections in Chapter 8 (The Extra Z). After several T-Spin Doubles and Tetrises, you will need to perform a Reset, which will take you back to the start of the Midgame. This cycle repeats until ST Stacking terminates, which usually happens due to player error.

 

Now lets take a quick look at the anatomy of ST Stacking.

The ST Stack can be divided into three regions during the Midgame:

Source: https://tl.net/forum/games/175252-10000-guide-tetris-ultra-st-stacking

 

●     The Left is Columns 1 through 4. This region will mostlybut not exclusivelybe filled by O, J, and I pieces.

●     The Center is Columns 5 through 7. This region will mostly be filled with SL pairs and OJ pairs. These pairs will be explained more in Chapter 3.

●     The Right is Columns 8 through 10. This region will exclusively be filled with Z, I, and T pieces. Here is where youll score your T-Spin Doubles and Tetrises!

 

99% of the time, a tetromino will not cross into two regions during the Midgame; a piece will usually be placed exclusively in the Left, Center, or Rightthough we will consider the other 1% of cases in Chapter 10 (Advanced Overhangs). Technically, the T pieces performing the T-Spin Doubles do cross the region borders, but we wont be concerned with them at all.

 

I will also be using some more terms in this guide:

●     The Pocket is the tall hole in Column 10. The height of the Pocket is determined by how tall the Column 9 red minos are, without the Z piece placed on top of it.

○     When the Z piece is covering the Pocket, the Pocket is capped.

○     When there is no Z piece covering the Pocket, the Pocket is uncapped.

■     For example, the Pocket in the screenshot above is capped and has a height of 7.

●     The Well is Column 8.

 

Chapter 2: The Opener

Finally! Were starting!

 

The Opener helps set you up for the ST Stacking pattern. Each Opener must satisfy each of the following criteria:

●     Exactly 2 Ts are used in the Left or Center

●     Exactly 1 Z is used in the Left or Center

●     1 Z is used in the Right to set up the first TSD (which would be performed by the 3rd T piece)

Furthermore, good Openers will also satisfy the following additional criteria:

●     An equal number of S and Ls are placed when the Center is flat. This means no hanging S or L pieces; more on that in Chapter 3 (The Midgame) and Chapter 7 (The Hanging S)

●     The Left and Center are about the same height.

I have compiled a list of Openers you can memorize and perform if you dont want to worry about freestyling and satisfying each of the bullet points listed above. These 5 or so Openers should cover most of your starting bags.

 

Spreadsheet Link: ST Stacking Openings

Note that Openings A and E require another T on the Left, and another Z on the Left or Center

 

The way to read the list of Openers is to pay special attention to the tetrominoes that are touching the floor, since these are the pieces that youll be looking for at the start of your first bag. Once youve found a corresponding Opener, emulate it as much as you can. Dont worry too much about copying the piece placements on the Left that are not touching the ground, but do copy the floor pattern and the first overhang to increase your chances of success.

 

If you just came here to learn ST Stacking and really dont like the idea of memorizing several openings, thats perfectly normal! I would recommend picking up Opening A for now and simply restarting whenever the first bag doesnt work for it. As you get more seasoned with ST Stacking, youll likely have the urge to get into the Midgame without as many restarts. At that point, I would advise learning another Opening one at a time, in the order of Openings C, D, B, and E.

 

Once youve completed your first T-Spin Double, congratulations! You have successfully transitioned into the Midgame.

 

Chapter 3: The Midgame

Here comes the fun part!

 

Since the Midgame is such a big chunk of the ST Stacking timeline, Ill need to split this chapter into 3 main parts, each part corresponding to a region of the stack.

 

Chapter 3.1: The Right
First, well cover the Right since its the least complex. If there is a Z piece in Columns 9 and 10, then drop a T-piece into Columns 8 and 9 to perform a T-Spin Double. Otherwise, place a Z piece to set up the next TSD. Each time you place a Z and perform a TSD, the Pocket will grow taller by 1.

If the Pocket has a height of at least 3, you can insert an I-piece into the Pocket or Well. Note that if you have the option to put an I-piece into the Pocket or Well, always try to put the I-piece into the Pocket first. This is because the Well is always open to accept I-pieces (given that the Well is tall enough). However, the Pocket can be blocked by Z pieces, so its optimal to fill the harder, Column 10 first if its available.

When given the option, use the I-piece to fill the Pocket (Column 10) before the Well (Column 8).

 

Remember that I-pieces are also used on the Left. I will discuss whether to put the I-piece in the Right or the Left later in Chapter 5 (The Inconvenient I Piece).

 

Chapter 3.2: The Center

Next, well cover the Center, which is where we create the overhangs for the TSDs. The Center consists of particular 3-by-3 overhangs that are stacked on top of each other.


If youve watched ST Stacking, youll know that the SL pair is the most common way to create these overhangs.

Three SL pairs

Since this pattern is used so much, the player has to keep track of their S/L parity.

Good S/L parity means that each bag successfully creates one SL pair.

Bad S/L parity means that within a bag, one of the tetrominoes was placed in the Center and the other was placed on the Left. This can hinder your ability to create SL pairs in the following bags. Ill explain this problem more in-depth in Chapter 7 (The Hanging S), but you should avoid bad S/L parity and fix it ASAP by dumping extra S or L pieces on your Left when it occurs.

 

The second most common way to create center overhangs is the OJ pair. This can be used when the player wants to raise the Center by creating 2 overhangs with 1 bag.

An OJ pair

The next overhang is the LO pair, which is not used as frequently as the previous two overhangs.

An LO pair

This overhang is usually created when the player is unable to place the S tetromino in the Center before the L. While this is a valid overhang, it creates bad S/L parity because the bag still contains an unaccompanied S in it. This unaccompanied S is called a Hanging S. The player should do their best to accommodate for the Hanging S on the Left so that they can return to good S/L parity and normal ST Stacking.

 

 

 

 

The last overhang is the ZJ pair.

A ZJ pair

This overhang is rarely used, since it utilizes a Z tetromino. Although I said that the Z belongs on the Right, there is a special case to use a Z piece outside of the Right region during the Midgame, but I will explain this special case in Chapter 8 (The Extra Z). Otherwise, this overhang should only be used in the Opener and the Reset.

If you look back to the list of Openers, this is a common way to create the first overhang. This ensures that there is a suitable place for the Z, since it can be difficult to find one elsewhere.

Another thing to note in the ZJ pair is that the order of which tetromino you place doesnt matter. If you place the Z first, then you can easily place the J next to it. If you place the J first, you can usually tuck the Z under it, assuming you havent built another overhang on the J already.

 

I like to call these four pairs The Big Four. These are all the ways you can create an overhang with 2 tetrominoes and comprise a large majority of my Center overhangs.

I will cover the other rare cases in Chapter 10 (Advanced Overhangs), where you could use more than 2 tetrominoes to create one or multiple overhangs at once. However, good ST Stacking will try to keep the overhangs simple whenever possible, so The Big Four are very important to remember.

 

Finally, well cover the Left. Hopefully your brain is still intact!

 

Chapter 3.3: The Left

Unlike the Right and Center, there isnt really much of a formula to follow. Its mostly freestyle, though how you freestyle on the Left can make or break your stacking.

 

If youve watched or played some ST Stacking, youll notice that the Left is never completely flat; there are always 2 minos that prevent the 4 leftmost columns from being the same height. This is perfectly normal; in fact we will only be looking at Left configurations with these two minos and creating strategies from them since they will never disappear.

 

Oftentimes, its best to model your Left as much as you can to your next queue. However, there are also certain patterns that you can rely on as they can accommodate most pieces. Chapter 4: The Reset

No, not that kind of reset, turn your computer back on!

 

If you ST stack long enough, youll notice that the column of red blocks from Z pieces will rise relative to the Left/Center of your stack.

The space between the Left/Center and the Right slowly shrinks!

 

This is bad, since the red column will eventually catch up to the Left or Center of the stack, which prevents you from performing T-Spin Doubles. This is where you will perform a strategy called the Reset.

 

The Reset is a technique that places 2 T pieces and 2 Z pieces (or 1 Z piece, more on that in Chapter 8) on the Center and Left of the stack. The reason for using 2 T and 2 Z pieces is that dumping only one T in the Left creates bad mino parity. (I wont go into detail about bad mino parity here, but placing a single T piece on the Left makes it jagged and difficult to place O and J pieces on top of it. Also, I am using the term mino parity instead of simply saying parity to distinguish this from S/L parity.) The idea behind Resetting is that you create more Center overhangs with 2 bags so that the red column doesnt rise above it. A successful Reset takes the player back to the Midgame, and the ST Stacking loop continues.

 

However, performing the Reset is not that simple; several problems can arise. First, placing a single T piece on the Left immediately creates bad mino parity. This makes it difficult to place the other tetrominoes on it, so ideally youll want to hold on to your first T for a while so that you can place the 2 T pieces together in quick succession.

Additionally, youll need to know when to Reset. Timing the Reset is very important: Reset too early and your Center and Left will rise too high and youll risk topping out; Reset too late and you may not have a comfortable Center and Left to dump your pieces on. Topping out from having a high Left and Center is a real risk, so its a good idea to Tetris as soon as possible after the Reset. If youre playing a gamemode that counts the number of cleared lines, your first Reset should come after 40 line clears, but Resetting at 50 line clears will give you much more breathing room when youre upstacking the Left and Center.

 

Additionally additionally, you will need to do your best to maintain your S/L parity. Ill explain how to fix S/L parity in Chapter 7 (The Hanging S), but a good Reset should keep your S/L parity balanced so you can transition straight into the Midgame in good condition after resetting.

 

Additionally additionally additionally, it is very tempting to build the Left much higher than the Center, since its difficult to create the Center overhangs with the 2 T pieces. However, a dangerously high Left forces you to place your next tetrominoes in the Center or Right. This is a problemespecially in high gravitybecause it limits your options, which could terminate your ST Stacking if you get an unfavorable sequence of pieces to dump in the Center. You have to do your best to keep the height of your Left and Center the same, or better yet the Center slightly higher with OJ or other overhangs, so that you have access to all parts of the matrix.

 

If all of these warnings seem daunting for the Reset, dont worry! Its reasonable to take a lot more time to think about where the pieces should go since theres no set formula like the Opener or Midgame. Ill have a strategy in Chapter 8 (The Extra Z) that will help make the Reset go by faster with fewer pieces, so hang tight!

 

At this point in the guide, youve learned basic ST Stacking! I highly recommend that you take a day or two to practice on your own and read up on the resources in Chapter 0. You will certainly run into problems with ST Stacking, but I think it will make more sense if you can find these problems then troubleshoot them using this guide, rather than learning about them before encountering them. Right now, do your best to get as far into the Midgame as possible, and then use the following chapters to help push you further into it until your first Reset! I dont want to overload you with too much information all at once, since each Chapter contains crucial information.

 

Chapter 5: The Inconvenient l Piece

OiI

 

For the next few chapters, well focus on specific tetrominoes that could cause problems. Well start with the easier pieces to resolve, then move towards peskier pieces.

 

The I-piece is usually the simplest to resolve; it either goes on the Left or the Right. If the I-piece goes Left, youll drop it in either Columns 1, 2, 3, or 4, and if the I-piece goes Right, youll drop it in either the Well or the Pocket. The I-piece rarely ever gets laid flat or laid in the Center because itll usually create holes, which is a big no-no. There are very few cases where the I-piece does get placed flat or in the Center, but these cases are rare and will be covered in Chapter 10 (Advanced Overhangs).

 

So, lets say you have an I-piece coming up. Do you put it on the Left or Right? Lets go through a few easy situations.

●     If the Pocket isnt tall enough, then put the I-piece on the Left (or hold it if the Pocket is just about to be tall enough).

●     If the Pocket is capped and theres no room to place it in the Well, then put the I-piece on the Left (or hold it until the Pocket opens up).

●     If you want to decrease the height of your board, place your I-piece on the Right.

Reminder: If you do choose to place your I-piece on the Right and both the Well and Pocket are empty, always attempt to put it in the Pocket before the Well. There are very few hard and fast rules in ST Stacking, and this is one of them. This is because putting the I-piece in an empty Well first will always give you fewer options than putting it in an empty Pocket.

 

Okay, so lets say youre not at risk of topping out, and your Pocket is tall enough. Left or Right?
In most cases, placing the I-piece in either location should be fine; do whatever feels most comfortable! However, if youve ST Stacked long enough, youll know that it isnt always obvious where to put the I-piece on the Left, and placing the I-piece on the Right is simply not possible.

It can be difficult to know where to put the I-piece sometimes...

 

In these cases, your best bet is to quickly scan your next queue and do your best to visualize a few moves ahead to see where the I-piece would best fit. But oftentimes, these awkward situations can be prevented in the first place!

Remember that each bag contains 1 I-piece. This may sound like I-pieces are scarce, but they often feel much more abundant in ST Stacking. Thus, you must accommodate them on your Left if your Right isnt ready.

 

This leads to a general rule of thumb:
If the Right cant accept I-pieces, always make an I-dependency on the Left with the rest of your tetrominoes, even if there is no I-piece coming up soon.
Also if you need I-pieces on your Right immediately, dont make I-dependencies on the Left lol

 

So what counts as an I-dependency on the Left? Clearly, any holes at least 3 minos deep is an I-dependency. However, some 2-mino deep holes will also work!

 

Tall holes easily accept I-pieces...

 

But so do 2-mino deep holes!

 

These 2-mino deep holes work because when an I-piece is placed in these holes, it leads to a healthy Left. You could also make these 2-mino deep holes in Columns 2 or 3, but this is less recommended since the Left will feel a bit cramped (although if your next queue has a good follow-up, then go for it!). I-dependencies are crucial since not making them will often lead to a jagged or spikey Left, which will make it difficult to place J, O, or even S or L pieces there.

 

There are also situations where the next queue gives you two I-pieces close to each other, but you havent built out two dependencies for them. Placing the Is vertically next to each other on the Left can also be a good idea, so long as you dont leave the Left too jagged.

In the first screenshot, the I-pieces can go in Cols 3 and 4

First, its best to always build your Left flat in some area. This can include:

●     a 3x2 rectangle on the Left

●     a 2x2 slot on the Left

●     a shallow valley in adjacent columns

All of these arrangements are almost always safe for placing the O, except for the center valley setup. For that setup, it is only recommended if a J is coming immediately afterwards; otherwise it can be somewhat difficult to place pieces around that elevated ground. You could also place the O in Columns 1 and 2, then spin a hanging S under it (more on that in the next Chapter)!

 

Note that how well the Left can accommodate the O is often indicative of the health of the Left. In other words, if the Left can safely accept O pieces, then youre freestyling well! This is because out of the 3 tetrominoes that usually go hereI, J, and OO tends to be the most troublesome. Thus, if you can fit O tetrominoes, you are in good shape! This is also part of the reasoning behind each setups ranking in the tierlist in Chapter 3.3!

 

Lets say that despite your best efforts, your Left is still too jagged or messy to accept O-pieces. The best-case scenario is that you can create an overhang on the Left and quickly tuck an upcoming piece to resolve it. In other cases, you will be forced to drop the O in Columns 5 and 6.

Chapter 7: The Hanging S

I wonder how many people skipped straight to this Chapter in the guide 🤔

 

After mentioning the hanging S for a bit earlier in this guide, it is finally time that we talk about it!

As I mentioned in Chapter 3.2, the SL pair is the most common overhang pair in the Center. This is notable for 2 reasons: the SL pair is a reliable way to build up the Center, but it also provides a place for the S piece to go for each bag.
Out of all the tetrominoes that usually go in the Left and Center during the Midgame (S, L, I, J, and O), the S is the most troublesome due to its odd geometry. L, I, J, and O pieces tend to fit well together, but the S tetromino can be difficult to place if you dont plan a place for it. It requires a bumpy floor and creates a bumpy surface on top when placed.

 

Luckily, the SL pair is our saving grace since it uses the awkward S geometry for overhangs. However, there will certainly be times when you put an early L in your hold queue and the S comes too late in the bag, but you need to use your Hold queue for something else. In situations like these, you will need to place the L first in the Left or Center, which will forfeit the SL pair for that bag and leave you with an unaccompanied S. And this is the infamous hanging S.

 

If youve attempted ST Stacking at all, youve certainly run into this problem before. While you can try to continue ST Stacking with a hanging S, it often wont go well; youve likely had an S placed in the Center, with another S in play having nowhere else to go on the board. The hanging S wont necessarily terminate ST Stacking, but the longer it persists, the more likely it will terminate. Thus, good ST Stackers will attempt to avoid the hanging S if possible, and will resolve them as fast as possible when it is absolutely necessary to make one.

 

The most common solution for this is to make space for the hanging S on the Left, since the Center is pretty narrow, and you still have to worry about making overhangs there. However, since the Left is the freestyle section and is the widest region, it lends itself to having the most solutions for resolving the hanging S. There are additional ways to get rid of the hanging S by using the Center, but these rare cases will be covered in Chapter 10 (Advanced Overhangs).

Chapter 8: The Extra Z

Dont sleep on the Z!

 

Although you can ST Stack without any placing Zs in the Left or Center during the Midgame, youll find that placing one can make your stacking go a lot smoother! You can avoid tricky Midgame positions, do more TSDs before the Reset, and make your Resets shorter and easier! This one simple trick can significantly increase the consistency of your ST Stacking!! Doctors hate this!!

 

Heres how the Extra Z works: any time during the Midgame, you are allowed to place exactly ONE Z piece in the Left or Center. No more, no less.

So why the big fuss about this one adjustment?

If you place the extra Z, youll notice that you can still ST Stack normally for a long time. Youll have a T piece in your Hold queue more often, but you can carry on with your TSDs and Tetrises almost like normal. What really matters is that your Midgame can be slightly extended. By placing additional minos in the Left and Center, youre increasing the vertical gap between the Left/Center and the Right (see the images in the start of Chapter 4), thus giving you more TSDs to play with! This means that if youre struggling with Resets, you can get slightly more mileage out of your run; and if you can perform Resets, it makes the Reset so much easier. Why?


Remember that in traditional ST Stacking, you need to dump 2 Z and T pieces on the Left or Center. While youre dumping these pieces, chances are that youre also dumping other minos that are not Z and T pieces down, which can raise your Left and Center to dangerous heights, which can then lead to an unfortunate topout. Some ST Stackers also struggle with maintaining a clean stack during the Reset; they will have trouble and create holes on the Left since Resetting has no set formula.

However, with this adjustment, you only need to dump 1 Z and 2 T pieces. As a result, you will end up dumping fewer pieces during the Reset, and you can begin the Reset with a lower board. By making the Reset shorter, you wont have to work with as many pieces and youll have a smaller risk of topout.
The bottom line: If you are struggling with Resets, this is your golden ticket!

 

In addition, having the option to place a Z in the Midgame gives you more flexibility! If you have a capped Pocket and have a Z in Hold, you now have the option to free up your Hold queue by placing an Extra Z! This can help you navigate out of uncomfortable Midgames. Moreover, if you want to quickly raise your Centerwhich may be desirable in high gravitythe Extra Z can be an answer to that problem with a ZJ pair!


You could place the L and J on the Left, but this will create an awkwardly shaped Left and a hanging S.

However, a ZJ pair in the Center will allow you to cleanly stack the SL pair above it!

 

Since the Extra Z is such an important concept, well revise our timeline from Chapter 1 to accommodate it.

Note that the Extra Z point in the timeline acts as a slider which can be placed anywhere between the Opener and Reset. While you can technically place your Extra Z as early or late into the Midgame as youd like, I would recommend that you perform it later, since having the option of placing the Extra Z grants you additional flexibility. It can act as a way out in a dire situation. Of course, if placing the Extra Z would avoid an awkward Midgame, then that is also a good move. Just be aware that if you place the Extra Z, you dont have this flexibility anymore, and you will need to play the rest of the Late Midgame as cleanly as you can until the Reset.

 

One small caveat of the Late Midgame is that the tierlist from Chapter 3.3 doesnt apply as well, since a T will be occupying your hold queue a lot; building out your Left and Center is going to feel different. As a result, there will be many times where you cant build SL pairs. This situation is not run-ending, but you will need to be comfortable with making OJ pairs, or making LO pairs and quickly resolving the hanging S. If things get really dangerous, you can choose to Reset early if you need to dispose of Ts right away, but this should only be used as a last resort.

 

Chapter 9: Remembering What Youve Forgotten

Because our monkey brains forget stuff lol

 

This Chapter is a direct follow-up to Chapters 7 and 8, so read up on those Chapters if you havent yet!

 

Oftentimes during ST Stacking, you might forget whether or not you have a hanging S or placed the Extra Z down. This information is crucial to remember, since you dont want to create 2 hanging S pieces or preemptively begin a Reset when youre not ready. However, were only human and forgetting these bits of info is a very real possibility when we have to deal with the chaos that is ST Stacking, especially after Resetting.

So lets say you forgot the status of your S and Z pieces. What can we do?

 

The short answer is that you need to be lucky and observant with the next queue. However, the more observant you are, the less luck you need to rely on. Dont worry, Ill explain what that means.

 

First, Ill need to introduce a new concept called flow. Flow is the idea of placing tetrominoes strictly in the order from oldest to newest. Another way to think about this is to imagine placing the block thats in your hold queue, then placing the rest of the pieces in order. Heres an example:

 

In this screenshot, assuming the player didnt hold the T piece just now, the flow of the tetrominoes would be T, Z, J, I, L, O, and S in that order.


Heres how well use flow to identify whether or not there is a hanging S.

●     If flow gives you an S piece when there is a lone S in the center
OR if flow gives you 2 S pieces with no L in between them or able to be held, then you do have a hanging S.

●     If flow gives you an L piece before an S piece when there is no lone S in the center, then you do not have a hanging S.

 

Lets analyze the first bullet point.

Situation 1: If S is your current piece when you have another lone S in the center, that means you placed the lone S in the previous bag and entered a new bag afterwards. This is the definition of a hanging S.

Situation 2: If you are dealt 2 S pieces with no L in between them or in the hold, then the flow will inevitably lead you back to Situation 1, meaning that you have a hanging S.

 

If you are a really observant ST Stacker, then identifying Situation 2 is much more powerful than Situation 1. This is because the earlier you know that you have a hanging S, the more time you have to adjust your Left to accommodate for it. Remember, it's preferable to resolve hanging S pieces as soon as possible to avoid breaking ST Stacking.

 

For the first screenshot, you can use Situation 2 and determine that theres a hanging S before the first S piece arrives. The two upcoming S pieces dont have an L between them, and theres no L piece that can be held before then.
For the second screenshot, the L piece is coming before the S piece when there is no lone S in the center. Thus, we know that there is no hanging S in that board.

 

Note that flow could also hide whether or not you have a hanging S by continuously alternating between S and L pieces.

 

We can also use flow to identify whether or not the Extra Z has been placed.

 

●     If flow gives you a Z piece before a T piece when the Pocket is already capped
OR if flow gives you 2 Z pieces with no T in between them or able to be held, then you have not placed the Extra Z yet.

●     If flow gives you a T piece before a Z piece when the Pocket is uncapped, then you have placed the Extra Z already.

 

The reasoning for these bullet points is the same as for the hanging S, but using the Z and T pieces in place of the S and L pieces. Lets go through two examples again.

 

For the first screenshot, there are two Z pieces in the flow with no T pieces in between them or able to be held before the Z pieces arrive. Thus, the Extra Z has not been placed yet.

For the second screenshot, there is a T piece in flow before a Z piece when the Pocket is uncapped. Thus, the Extra Z has been placed already.

 

Again, if flow continuously alternates between T and Z pieces, then you wont be able to tell if the Extra Z has been placed from the bullet points alone.

 

Remember that you can also look for other indicators to determine hanging S pieces or Extra Z pieces. For example, if you see two consecutive I pieces immediately coming in the next queue and have an S in play, you know that you have a hanging S since youre entering a new bag. The four bullet points in this chapter are not the only indicators.

Also, this Chapter assumes that there is only 1 hanging S or 1 Extra Z in question; these rules dont apply well when there are 2 hanging S or 2 Extra Z pieces in question. However, having 2 hanging S pieces or placing 2 Extra Z pieces is extremely rare, and hopefully you will already know that when you enter these situations instead of looking for indicators.

 

Chapter 10: Advanced Overhangs

For when the Big Four dont work and you want to look super cool 😎

 

So lets say your upcoming next pieces are filled with I, O, and J tetrominoes, but your Left is either too high, too unaccommodating, or too unwieldy to place any of them there. None of your Big Four setups will work, and things are looking pretty dire.

For whatever reason, the Left is way too tall and the O already fell too far to place it there... oops.
Luckily, there is still a way out without breaking the back-to-back!

 

This is where advanced overhangs come in! Advanced overhangs can be difficult to see, but they often mean the difference between continuing and breaking ST Stacking. They involve the player to stack at least 2 tetrominoes outside of the standard Big Four overhangs, which means that these tetrominoes will either be extending vertically to create multiple overhangs at once, or extending horizontally to the Left. Well look at both cases separately.

 

First, well cover two vertical extensions which both make two overhangs! These are super useful for quickly raising your Center and resolving hanging Ss!

The OSJJ and OSIO extension overhangs!

I briefly showed the OSIO extension in Chapter 6 since its an efficient way to dump 2 Os quickly when the Left is in a really bad shape. The OSJJ extension can also work if you dont have an I-piece at your disposal, but have a surplus of J tetrominoes. This is the solution for the screenshot above! Since both of these setups start with an O in Columns 5 and 6, this opens up many more options to resolving Center Os, in addition to the OJ pair!
If youve already placed the OS formation in the Center, do your best not to stack any tetrominoes on top of them until you know you can finish it off with an OSJJ or OSIO extension. For example, if you place the 2nd O in the OSIO extension, you will become very dependent on the I-piece to finish it off. This is great if an I-piece is coming up, but certainly not good if your next queue wants you to make more overhangs in the Center immediately. A similar warning can be made for preemptively putting the 1st J in the OSJJ extension, although this brings us to a very interesting kind of super-extension.

 

OSJJ, but without a J?

 

This is what I like to call the OSJ module. Ideally, you would want another J to finish off the OSJJ extension, but you can go even further!

OSJS?! Shocky, what the heck are you doing?!

 

If the next queue is really giving you a hard time with S pieces, who says that you cant dump another one on top of the J? While this move may seem wild (especially compared to our relatively tame Big Four), this OSJS formation actually circles back to our initial OS formation! Think about it: you can still top off the OSJS with either I and O tetrominoes or 2 J tetrominoes (thus giving us OSJSIO and OSJSJJ extensions) to return back to a flat Center and normal ST Stacking. If youre really crazy, you can add another JS into the mix for an OSJSJS_ _ extension and so on. But usually at this point, it gets really excessive and unnecessary since youre now stacking L pieces on the Left and straying really far from the conventional ST Stacking formula. If you can pull this off, then cool! Just bear in mind that your Center is going to rise super fast, and that returning to normal ST Stacking is still your top priority.

 

Another quick vertical extension that Id like to cover is the ZJ extension (not to be confused with the ZJ pair!)

A ZJ extension! But somethings missing...


The ZJ extension can be useful if youve accidentally placed minos in Column 5 that would otherwise block your Big Four pairs from working. However, keep in mind that the J tetromino needs another layer of minos on top of it to satisfy the overhang shape. In this screenshot, placing an I in Column 5 and an L in Columns 5-7 will work. However if your Column 5 has already been filled in by some stray minos by accident, youll need to find more creative ways to fill that row on top of the J with freestyling.

Alternatively, you could continue building on top of this narrow extension, thus making a ZJLSO extension, like this:

A ZJLSO extension. What a mouthful!

 

For this type of extension, you will need to build out your Left and Column 5 very carefully so that the height in Column 5 will be the exact height as the top surface of the O in Columns 6-7. This can be difficult, so keeping an eye on your next queue is absolutely key! If youre successful, you can then return back to normal ST Stacking. Note that you could also break the ZJLSO extension early if you can find a way to create a flat Center immediately (For example, you can place a 180-spun J in the Center, on top of a ZJL segment. Note that Column 5 must also be at a suitable height).

Remember that any variant of the ZJ extension will count as placing your Extra Z, or count as initiating the Reset if the Extra Z has already been placed before.

 

Now well cover two horizontal extensions! I swear that these wont be as complicated.

The SJI and SSI horizontal extensions!

 

Unlike vertical extensions, these horizontal extensions only create one overhang, so your stack isnt going to rise as much. The SJI extension is pretty straightforward: it discards a hanging S with the help of a J and an I piece. The SSI extension gets rid of 2 S tetrominoes. If youve had a hanging S before the SSI extension was built, then creating this extension will leave you with a hanging L, which I usually put next to the extension. You could also use it to make an LO pair on top of the extension or just dump an L on the Left later whenever you see fit!
This is also a reason why you should build the Center slightly higher than your Left during the Midgame: it allows you to make horizontal extensions, thus giving you more flexibility to continue ST Stacking when the next queue gets really dicey. If the Left is much taller than the Center, you wont have room to build these extensions, and your options are slightly reduced.

 

Remember that you can always try to freestyle or create your own patterns outside of these examples, but good ST Stackers will do their best to return to a normal Midgame as soon as possible. Again, the Big Four should always be the bread and butter of your ST Stacking; advanced overhangs should only come into play if they create a more favorable stack (resolving hanging Ss, quickly raising the Center, etc.) or if they provide a way out of what would have otherwise been a dead end.

 

Chapter 11: Feeling the Bag and 3-Piece Preview

In case you wanted to do more mental exercise on top of keeping track of S and Z pieces

 

If youre playing a version of Tetris with a 5-piece preview, then this chapter may not be as helpful. However, in versions with 3-piece previews like Tetris Mobile or Tetris.com, this chapter is going to be much more important. This will be one of the more abstract and shorter chapters in this guide, but I can assure you that the information here is still very useful!

If youre playing with a 3-piece preview or dont look at the next queue very often (like me), there are ways to feel your way through a bag to see what kinds of pieces are likely to come up next.
Remember that a bag contains each of the 7 tetrominoes. Well split these tetrominoes into 3 groups based on where each of them are commonly placed: IOJ, SL, and ZT. If you pick one of these groups and track which of the tetrominoes in the group have and havent been placed, you can get a sense of how youre progressing through the bag.

 

In my experience, keeping track of the SL pair works best since it strikes a balance between simplicity and utility. Lets go through an example in the Midgame.


Lets assume that youre building SL pairs in the Center and that there are no hanging S or L pieces.

●     If the Center is flat, this means that youll likely place an SL pair in the middle soon. Thus, you are either exiting your previous bag or you are entering a new bag.

●     If the bag has dispensed an S or an L but not both, this means that youre in the middle of a bag.

●     If youve placed both the S and L pieces, youre getting close to or are already at the end of the bag. This situation is identical to the first bullet point.

So why does keeping track of your bag location matter? If youve tried to speedrun ST Stacking before, youll know that it can be hard to accommodate the O and I pieces when there isnt a space for them, and it may not always be easy to keep a space open for both of them. By keeping tabs of where you are, you can get away with a temporarily jagged Left if youve already placed the O from the current bag already, and a similar logic can be applied to I-pieces.

 

I personally prefer keeping track of the SL group. This is because the tetrominoes you need to keep track of in the ZT pair changes when the Extra Z is placed, and I sometimes forget if Ive placed it or not. Additionally, it requires me to glance at the Right, which may be situated much lower than the Center or Left, which costs time during Ultra runs. I choose not to keep track of the IOJ group because it can be a bit unwieldy to track three tetrominoes, and the extra bit of information you do get out of it isnt very helpful. Again, the SL pair strikes a nice balance of simplicity and utility for me. Of course, you can choose a different group from me, or create a completely different group to track that makes you feel comfortable! However, keeping track of any group of minos is undoubtedly advantageous! Combined with a 3-piece preview, this strategy will give you a lot more insight and confidence into which tetrominoes are coming up next.

 

One last thought to leave you with: Is it possible to infinitely ST Stack with a 1-piece preview? Of course youll need to keep track of where exactly you are in each bag, but I wonder if this is enough information to continue stacking forever. I suppose Ill leave this as an exercise for the reader \_(ツ)_/

 

Chapter 12: High Gravity and 20G Adaptations

For the really darn sweaty players out there o_o

 

In high gravity, pieces fall much faster than normal, which can limit how far left or right a piece can go if you have a bumpy stack. However, Tetris.com and Tetris Effect introduce the hardest gravity setting: 20G. In 20G, the pieces dont even fall anymore and instead spawn on the top of your stack! If youve heard of any 20G strategies, youll know that pyramid stacking is a common solution, where the center of a stack is built higher than the rest in order to slide pieces downwards as they move towards the left or right of the board. Well use this idea to our advantage for high gravity and 20G!

When playing in 20G, it is absolutely necessary to keep your Center slightly higher than the Left. If the Left ever becomes higher than the Center, it will be impossible to place pieces on the Left, and you will be forced to dump pieces either on the Center or Right, which is a good recipe for ST Stacking disaster. On the other hand, the Right is always lower than the Center automatically, so we wont need to worry about the Right region of the board in 20G.

 

My 20G stacking habits vary significantly from my low-gravity stacking habits. Oftentimes, you will need to put much more emphasis on balancing the height difference between the Left and Center. Ill give two examples of when this balancing act matters.

In the first screenshot, instead of making an SL pair in the Center, we should place the S, L, and J pieces on the Left to give it extra height. If the SL pair is made, then we will be forced to take a T-Spin that clears no lines.

In the second screenshot, instead of placing the J in columns 2-4 and the O in columns 3-4, we should make an OJ pair to give the Center extra height. The reason for using the OJ pair is that it is preferable to have a slightly higher Center, since it gives us more placement options for upcoming pieces. If the J and O are placed on the Left, then we are forced to place the next O in the Center due to 20G, and it is not guaranteed that an S or J piece will come soon to continue off the Center O.

Remember that if your Left or Center needs an extra height boost immediately, you can place the Z on the Left, or a ZJ pair in the Center. Chances are, youll need to place the Extra Z much earlier than normal, so youll need to be proficient in ST Stacking during the Late Midgame.

As the Late Midgame approaches the Reset, you may need to slowly close the vertical gap between the Left and Center to continue scoring TSDs. This is a good sign that you should be looking for an opportunity to Reset. When you do decide to start dumping Z and T pieces, always ensure that your Center is the highest point of the board, even while Resetting!

 

In 20G, some pieces may kick weirdly, making it difficult to rotate and move then where you want them to go. Here are a few examples of the steps youll need to take in order to place these pieces in the right spot.

●     SL Pair

○     S - Move right once

○     L - Move right twice, then rotate CW twice.

●     OJ Pair

○     O - Hard drop!

○     J - Rotate CCW twice

●     LO Pair

○     L - Move right once, then rotate CW or IRS CW
(IRS = Initial Rotation System. Only available in Tetris Effect)

○     O - Move right once

●     ZJ Pair

○     Z (J not placed) - Move right once, then rotate CCW
or IRS CW, then move right once.

○     Z (J placed) - Move right twice, rotate either direction, then move left

○     J (Z not placed) - Move right once, then rotate CW or IRS CW

○     J (Z placed) - Rotate CW or IRS CW. Pick your poison. \_(ツ)_/

●     Right Z - DAS to the right wall, then rotate CCW thrice!

●     Right T - DAS To the right wall, then hold left while rotating CW twice

 

For the Left, youll simply need to tease out what sequence of inputs youll need to make, considering all of 20Gs quirks.

20G is significantly harder than regular ST Stacking, so dont tackle it until you feel that youve truly mastered it in low gravity! I would recommend completing a 400-line (about 1,000 placed tetrominoes) ST Stacking run in Jstriss Practice mode before diving into 20G!

 

Chapter 13: Reviving ST Stacking

This is what I like to call Primetime Panic stacking!

 

This part of the guide deals with misdrops or ST Stacking dead ends, but you still want to score significant points without restarting such as in Tetris Effects Score Attack or Tetris.com or Tetris Primetime ;w;!

 

If youre lucky and the misdrop only creates holes on the Left, you can attempt to preserve the ST Stack. Stack on top of these holes, then push them as far down as possible with well-timed TSDs and Tetrises. This way, the hole will act as a row of garbage that hopefully wont take up too much board space.

If there are holes on the Left, you can try to bury the holed row as deep as possible. In the first screenshot, it would be ideal to score the Tetris before the T-Spin. Then, do your best to ST Stack as normal!

 

Note that if the size of the hole spans an odd number of minos, your Left will have bad mino parity and youll have a really hard time stacking J and O pieces there. You can choose to send down another 1-mino hole to fix the mino parity and see how it goes, but keep in mind that youll be ST Stacking off of an even higher floor, making it harder to not topout.

If the size of the hole is even but not divisible by 4 minos, then youll have a different kind of Left that can be completely flat at times. ST Stacking is still viable, but keep in mind that youll be working with completely new Left patterns that may be unfamiliar. I would put another tierlist here with these new kinds of patterns if I could, but I dont think I have enough knowledge on it to make one, and this guide probably has enough tierlists already 😅

However, most of the time youll need to rebuild the ST Stack entirely from scratch. There are 4 steps to follow:

1. Downstacking

2. Building a Foundation

3. Improvise an Opener

4. Monitor S and Z pieces

Downstacking should be fairly straightforward! Once youve downstacked enough, youll need to make a foundation that satisfies two criteria:

●     Columns 8-10 have no minos in them

●     The number of minos remaining on the board is divisible by 4.

A good foundation!

 

Once this is accomplished, youll need to construct your own Opener from scratch. Remember that your improvised Opener must satisfy the three requirements listed in Chapter 2. As youre constructing the new Opener, youll need to keep a very close eye on your next queue since you may have started constructing your Opener in the middle of a bag. Once all of the Opener requirements have been satisfied, you will need to determine the status of your hanging S and Extra Z pieces with the information from Chapter 9. It is possible that the stack could have many hanging S or L tetrominoes, or that you may need to place another Z in the Left or Center in order to properly transition into the Early Midgame.

 

Executing this process is very difficult. The best way to practice ST revival is to go into a practice environment, then deaddropping a few pieces to give yourself some garbage to downstack. It requires much more brain power in the moment to revive ST Stacking, but mastering this technique will give you a huge advantage in competitive score attack modes!

Note that ST Stacking revival should only be learned once youve mastered the rest of ST Stacking! Its not a good idea to be relying on revives as a crutch for frequently bumping into dead ends.

No one really asked these questions, I just wanted a fun way to end this guide and a place to put info that wouldnt really fit in any chapter. Might add some more if I do get questions though!


Why is it called ST Stacking if the Z and T pieces stand out the most in ST Stacking?

lol i dunno, the first player who invented ST Stacking probably stacked S and T pieces on the Left, but I dont have any sources to verify that. However, most players and guides use Z and T pieces on the Right instead of S and T pieces on the left, so its easier to learn the ZT variant from them. One objective advantage of the ZT variant is that I-kicks are biased towards the left, so there is at least one case where an I-kick would work in the ZT variant, but not in the ST variant under high gravity.

The I-piece can kick into the slot on the left with a CCW rotation, but neither rotation will work on the right.

 

Another plausible reason for ZTs original popularity would be that the ZT variant plays on many players 9-0 Stacking instincts. By stacking in the standard 9-0 method, a player can perform a kaidan with the right stack shape, then transition into ZT Stacking temporarily. Again, this is just speculation.

 

Is ST Stacking the most efficient stacking method for points per block?
No, there is a more point-efficient method called LST Stacking, which can use every T piece for a TSD (unlike ST which wastes about 7.2% of T-pieces due to Resets and the Opener). However, there are even fewer guides written about LST Stacking and Im definitely not knowledgeable enough to write a guide about LST at the time of writing this.

 

There are also Perfect Clear loops that use PC Scoring to earn more points than ST, but this is highly dependent on how much they score on each game. They are worthwhile in Jstris Ultra, but not as much in Tetris Mobile because they are scored differently.

 

However, some infinite stacking techniques like LST start to fall apart at high gravity or 20G. This could mean that ST Stacking is the most efficient for high gravity score attacks like in Tetris.com or Tetris Effects Score Attack, but I dont have the complete knowledge to confirm this.

 

Some players seem to maintain ST forever, but I keep running into dead ends that terminate my ST Stacking. How can I get out of dead ends?

The number of objective dead ends in ST Stacking is much smaller than you may think. Im not sure if ST Stacking can be maintained infinitely, but in terms of probability Im sure that real dead ends come very rarely given Perfect Play. I have been able to complete a 1000L ST Stacking run without breaking the b2b bonus. If youre hitting dead ends consistently before the first Reset, try to record your games and look back at the replay and figure out what missteps led to the dead end. If you know which part of the bag youre in (even with a 3-piece preview), you can find a solution to continue ST in most cases.

 

 

 

Can you maintain ST forever with Perfect Play?

Thats a difficult question to answer. To new playersincluding me when I started learningit may seem that the answer is clearly No. However, looking back, Im not too sure. Id love to see a proof that can prove infinite play, but Im equally as interested to see a proof showing that infinite ST is impossible. Maybe if I have enough time, Ill work on these proofs, but that wont be for a while.
